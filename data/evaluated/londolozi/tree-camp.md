## Value
Tree Camp sells a single product — six identical "Tree Camp Suites" — so there is no
internal price ladder: every guest buys the same suite at the same per-person rate. A
couple's benchmark ADR is **ZAR 98,049 (≈ USD 5,265)** for eleven months of the year,
rising to **ZAR 108,175 (≈ USD 5,809)** for a mid-December arrival (the only month a
5-night stay crosses into the 2027 rate card — see Self-competitiveness). A group of
eight filling four suites pays **ZAR 392,196 (≈ USD 21,061)** ADR, or **ZAR 432,700
(≈ USD 23,236)** in December. A solo traveller in a suite pays **ZAR 61,137** ADR.

What that rate buys is genuinely all-in. The rate card lists as included "All meals,
teas, and coffees daily", "All alcoholic and non-alcoholic beverages (except
exclusions)", "Two game drives daily in open Land Rovers", "Nature walks accompanied by
experienced, armed rangers", airstrip transfers, "Emergency medical evacuation
insurance", "Laundry service", "In-room WIFI" and VAT — so the ADR is close to the true
nightly cost, not a stripped headline rate. Against the Benchmark Safari's "all meals +
one activity/day" minimum the camp **exceeds** the bar (two drives plus walks, all
drinks). The physical product is correspondingly high-end: each suite has a "Private
Plunge Pool", "contemplation decks (salas)", "Ralph Lauren wallpaper and chocolate
plaited leather beds" and the camp is a Relais & Châteaux member (dossier Website &
Wetu). The mandatory Guest Conservation Contribution (ZAR 957.50 pppn, capped at 3
nights) is the only line added on top of the rate, and it is itemised separately in the
ADR.

**Assumptions the pricing script had to make (each a real gap):**

- Two rate cards apply; each night priced under its own card (2026 through 15 Dec 2026, 2027 thereafter), so a 15-Dec stay straddles the boundary.
- No trade/STO rate published on the Londolozi cards; STO shown equal to RACK.
- Guest Conservation Contribution capped at 3 nights and applied to all guests (the card neither age-bands nor exempts children).
- Tree Camp takes no guests under 16 unless the camp is booked exclusively; exclusive-use buyout is not assumed, so under-16 parties are infeasible.
- Tree Camp Suite max occupancy 2 (couple suite; no third-person rate on the card).
- Park entrance fees, road transfers, the noise-impact levy and private vehicles are excluded under the benchmark's fly-in assumption.

The two ✗ marks above are checklist artefacts rather than true gaps. **Inclusions /
exclusions** are in fact present and detailed — the dossier's rate-card section carries
a full "Group-wide inclusions" and "Group-wide exclusions" list (the auto-checker's
keyword simply missed the words "inclusions/exclusions"). **Check-in / check-out** is
likewise documented — Wetu Fast Facts give "Check-In: 13:30 / Check-Out: 11:00" — but
sits outside the rate-card section the checker scans. The genuinely absent fields are
**minimum stay** (no minimum-night requirement appears anywhere on the card, so the
pricing script imposes none) and **cancellation terms** (the card defers to an
unquoted "Group Policy" — "Refer to our Group Policy for group rates, terms and
conditions" — which was not captured).

The substantive gaps are the pricing-script assumptions listed above. Two matter most.
First, **no trade/STO rate is published** anywhere on the Londolozi cards, so the
RACK and STO columns are identical — the "trade" figure is not a real discounted rate
but a stand-in (any agent commission is invisible in these numbers). Second, the camp's
single room type carries **no third-person rate**, so the suite is modelled at max
occupancy 2; this is consistent with the dossier ("Six private suites", couple-oriented
positioning) but means a third occupant cannot be priced. The conservation levy is
applied to all guests because the card neither age-bands nor exempts it — moot here, as
no under-16s are accepted.

## Fit
Tree Camp is squarely an **adults' couples-and-honeymoon camp**, and the rate card
enforces it: "Regrettably, no children under 16 years old … Children between the ages
of 6 and 16 years can only be accommodated if the entire camp is booked." Because a
camp buyout is not a standard booking, the Benchmark **Family persona (children 6, 10,
14) is infeasible in all 12 months** — the ADR table correctly shows 0/12 for families.
The dossier positioning reinforces the target guest: "a sanctuary of elegance and
simplicity", "a celebration of Leopards, Leadwoods and Lanterns", with guests pictured
"on the Tree Camp deck, champagne in hand, as elephants pass in the river below" and a
"Magical Honeymoon" among the reviews.

The six suites (each max 2) give a hard camp capacity of 12, which is why the **Group
persona of 8 adults is feasible** (four suites, 392,196 ADR) — a small multi-room
buyout works well for a friends' group or a family of adult children, but not for
anyone travelling with school-age children. The best fit is a couple seeking a
design-led, fully-inclusive Big-Five safari with no children present; the worst fit is
a multi-generational family with young kids, who are simply not catered for.

## Self-competitiveness
**Rack-vs-trade spread: none (0%).** No STO/trade rate is published, so RACK and STO are
equal in every cell — there is no captured wholesale margin to discuss.

**Seasonal spread: effectively flat, with one calendar artefact.** Each rate card
quotes a single year-round rate, so the Couple ADR is a constant ZAR 98,049 from January
through November. The only movement is **December (ZAR 108,175, +10.3%)** — and that is
not a peak-season surcharge but the mechanical effect of a 15-December arrival straddling
the card boundary: the first night prices on the 2026 card and the following four on the
higher 2027 card (R48,450 → R54,750 pps). The same +10.3% step appears in the Group row
(392,196 → 432,700). A guest arriving 1 December would still pay the November rate;
the benchmark's fixed 15th-of-month arrival is what surfaces the increase.

**Single-supplement burden: ~25%, as stated.** The card levies "a 25% single supplement
from date of arrival", and the numbers bear it out: a solo guest's ADR is ZAR 61,137
versus ZAR 49,024.50 per head for a sharing couple (half of 98,049) — a **24.7% premium**
(the small dilution below 25% is the flat per-person conservation levy). Solo travel is
penalised but not punitively so by ultra-luxury standards.

## Reputation

### Reputation summary

| Source | Rating | Reviews | Sample / span |
|---|---|---|---|
| TripAdvisor | 4.9 / 5 | 171 total | 19 quoted (partial, top-sorted) |

TripAdvisor shows a stated overall of **4.9 / 5 across 171 total reviews**. That headline
is authoritative, but the **19 reviews actually quoted are a partial, top-sorted sample**
(roughly the first two pages), so they describe the top of the distribution, not a
representative average. Within that sample the split is stark: **18 of the 19 are 5/5**,
and a single **1/5** outlier sits at the bottom — so the captured distribution is almost
entirely top-box, by construction of how the sample was pulled.

Recency is strong: the quoted reviews run from a **May 2011** outlier through a dense
recent cluster, the newest being **Kristen0709 (Jun 2026)**, with several other 5/5
reviews from early 2026 (Andrew V, Feb 2026; Richard R, Jan 2026). The praise is
consistent and concrete — on the guiding ("our guide Patrick is a natural educator …
Dorence is such an experienced and well-trained tracker that it seemed like magic" —
Worldbutterfly, Dec 2025), the suites ("the Tree Camp suites are modern with large
picture windows … an outside deck with plunge pool and shower — an oasis of calm" —
eawnyc, Sep 2024), and the overall verdict ("Londolozi Tree Camp is the definition of
living up to the hype" — Worldbutterfly, Dec 2025; "my stay at Londolozi Tree Camp was
the most extraordinary travel experience I've ever had" — Richard R, Jan 2026).

The **only criticism in the captured data is the lone 1/5** from displomat (May 2011),
which complained that "The price for this safari was outrageous - the food was gross,
unprofessionally prepared and offered no options" and that game viewing "became very
redundant and boring." It is a single fifteen-year-old review and predates the camp's
renovation that a 2026 guest explicitly references ("excited to stay in Tree Camp again,
following the extensive renovations" — Andrew V, Feb 2026); no comparable complaint
appears elsewhere in the sample. Beyond that one outlier, **no recurring negative theme
is present in the captured reviews** — which, given the top-sorted sample, should be read
as "no complaints surfaced here", not as proof none exist across all 171.
